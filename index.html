    The placenta is the body's only disposable organ

    The placenta is made from the same cells that make the baby - sperm and egg. Some cells differentiate into the baby and others go on to form the placenta. The placenta does its job supporting and nourishing the baby and when it is no longer needed, it is expelled, making it the body's only disposable organ

    complications of child birth

    Failure to progress. Often called “prolonged labor,” failure to progress generally describes a situation in which active labor becomes protracted and stalls indefinitely. This could happen for a variety of reasons, including having contractions that aren’t strong enough; a cervix that’s stopped dilating; or a baby that is not descending down the birth canal despite pushing.

    While obstetricians in recent years have acknowledged that women in labor don’t need “to progress at a certain rate over time,” said Dr. Isaacs, your doctor may ultimately need to intervene if your labor is at an impasse despite trying to move things along non-invasively. This could mean a shot of medication called Pitocin (a synthetic hormone that can strengthen and speed up uterine contractions), an episiotomy (a surgical incision made to enlarge the vaginal opening) or the use of forceps to help deliver the baby. But some medical interventions, such as Pitocin, can come with risks, including an increased chance of a cesarean section (though the link between Pitocin and C-sections is being debated). Still, there has been a renewed focus on allowing women to labor longer — provided baby and mother are healthy — to avoid unnecessary medical procedures and to make vaginal births safer. How to prevent it. Because the transition to the hospital can be stressful and jarring, many practitioners recommended not rushing to the hospital at the very first sign of labor (unless you’re high risk or are having another medical emergency). The further along you are in labor before arriving at the hospital, said Dr. Isaacs, the more natural momentum your body will have to progress once you get there.

    C-section

    Cesarean section, C-section, or Cesarean birth is the surgical delivery of a baby through a cut (incision) made in the mother's abdomen and uterus. Health care providers use it when they believe it is safer for the mother, the baby, or both. The incision made in the skin may be: Up-and-down (vertical).

    pain level

    You won't feel any pain during the C-section, although you may feel sensations like pulling and pressure. Most women are awake and simply numbed from the waist down using regional anesthesia (an epidural and/or a spinal block) during a C-section. That way, they are awake to see and hear their baby being born.
